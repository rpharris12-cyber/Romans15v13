The Verse for the Year

Romans 15 verses 1 to 13 is about a church pulling apart — strong and weak, Jew and Gentile, real friction between people with different backgrounds and different ways of following Jesus. People who didn't naturally choose each other — and sometimes didn't like each other. Paul's call is simple and costly: bear with one another, welcome one another, because Christ has welcomed you.

Verse 13 is where all of that lands. Not a warm sign-off, but the fuel for everything he has just asked of them. And earlier this year, Ian opened it up for us around three movements: Jesus as the foundation of our hope through the cross, the focus of our hope as we trust him now, and the fulfilment of our hope in what is still to come.

Ian showed us the why. This morning I'd like us to sit with the how. Not the destination — the movement. What does it actually look like to be a person who overflows?

Pork Chop Sunday

Some of you will remember the illustration Ian used. Boaz — the dog — and pork chops. After Messy Church, the chops come out of the fridge. From that moment on, completely and joyfully fixed on what's coming.

But before Sunday morning service — when he knows he's going to be left — he won't settle. He paces. Same dog. Completely different posture. Which one looks more like us?

That pork chop expectation — joyful, certain, completely fixed on what's coming — that's what Ian was pointing us towards. Hold that picture of expectation as we hear these words again.

~1 min 30 sec

An Ordinary Cup of Tea

I often make tea in the morning. One morning I made the usual two cups, and as I tried to open the bedroom door with my elbow, I wobbled and the tea spilled. Not a disaster — just a few drips down the side of the cup, easily cleaned up.

There was nothing wrong with the cup, and nothing wrong with the tea (Rosie might differ on my tea-making skills). It spilled simply because the cup was full.

The verse for the year is not about becoming bigger, stronger, or more impressive people of faith. It is about being people who are filled — so that something quietly and naturally spills into the world around us. And Paul tells us exactly where that filling comes from.
Romans 15:13 · Phrase by Phrase
~1 min 30 sec

"May the God of Hope…"

Romans 15:13 is not an instruction, not a target, and not a test to pass. It is a prayer. Paul is not telling the church what they must achieve; he is praying for what God longs to do in them.

That matters — especially if faith feels fragile, uncertain, new, or simply ordinary. Let's step through Paul's prayer phrase by phrase.

Not the God who gives hope, but the God of hope. And Paul calls him that not simply because of his character — steady, faithful, unchanging — but because of what he has done. The cross. The resurrection. Every promise made to Israel, fulfilled in Christ, now opened to the whole world.

God did not stay at a safe distance and wish us well. He entered the mess — and proved himself the God of hope in history, not just in theory.

~1 minute

"As You Trust in Him"

Trust is not having everything worked out — it's choosing where you look. It is orientation — intentionally turning towards Jesus with what we have, not what we wish we had.

Think of the boy who brought five loaves and two fish — small, ordinary, not enough. He offered what he had. Jesus did the rest.

The turning is the moment. The moment your eyes move towards Jesus, that is when he begins to pour.

~1 minute

What Turning Might Look Like

So what might that actually mean on an ordinary busy day?

  • Prayer. Not polished words — just the turned face. Even a single word: help. That is a prayer. That is the turn.
  • Pause. A moment of deliberate stillness before the next thing. Long enough to stop filling yourself with noise, and to receive instead. Thirty seconds in the car before you go in.
  • Scripture. Not a study programme — one verse, held through the day. Let it steep. Let God's word do quietly what you cannot do for yourself.
These are not strict rules to follow or boxes to tick. It is simple, and it is personal — and it will look different for each of us. The only question is: where do you turn?
~1 min 30 sec

"Fill You with All Joy and Peace"

Notice that word — fill. Not earned, produced, nor maintained — fill.

Think of a cup being filled by a teapot — the cup doesn't fill itself. It simply sits beneath the spout and receives.

Joy here is not surface happiness but a deep gladness rooted in the knowledge that God is present and faithful. Peace is not the absence of difficulty; it is wholeness — life held together even when circumstances remain unresolved.

Sometimes it's sudden — you'll know when it happens. Often it's more like something steeping over time, so gradual you almost don't notice until you look back and realise something has changed.

Waiting without joy becomes despair. Waiting without peace becomes anxiety. God knows what sustained trust requires — so he fills us. Not so life becomes easy, but so waiting becomes possible.
~1 minute

"So That You May Overflow with Hope"

God does not fill us simply for our own comfort; overflow is the intention. But overflow is not something we force. It is the natural result of being filled.

  • Sometimes that overflow is barely noticeable — a drip.
  • Sometimes it is unmistakable — a waterall
  • Both matter.

Hope moves outward into patience we did not plan, kindness we did not rehearse, and conversations we did not script.

And it's worth asking — toward whom? Paul isn't writing this just to individuals. He's writing to a church learning to live together across real difference — strong and weak, Jew and Gentile, people who got on each other's nerves. The overflow of hope is what makes unity possible between people who wouldn't naturally choose each other.

~45 seconds

"By the Power of the Holy Spirit"

Paul finishes by lifting the weight from our shoulders: "by the power of the Holy Spirit."

Overflow is not personality, effort, or enthusiasm; it is the Spirit's work.

Which means we do not manufacture it — we make space for the Spirit to do what we cannot do for ourselves.
~45 seconds

Return to the Cup

  • An ordinary cup. Nothing special.
  • And because it is full, it spills — not because something has gone wrong, but because something has gone right.

Large or small, fine china or chipped mug, confident or uncertain, full of energy or feeling a little tired and worn — the vessel itself is of no concern.

What matters is not the size or shape of the vessel, but who is filling it. The only question is: will we bring what we have, and ask him to fill us afresh?
The Sending · This Week, This Month, This Year
~30 seconds

Where Is Your Splash Zone?

So — if that's what being filled looks like, what does the spilling look like?

Not where you have tried hard, or performed well, or managed to keep it together. But where something has simply spilled out of you — quietly, unexpectedly, without being planned — because you were full.

It will look different for each of us. Only you will know which of these is yours.

~30 seconds

Where Is Your Splash Zone?

The log flume. The dolphin show splash zone.

Perhaps this is the moment for you to make a big impact. You already know it — something bigger is in front of you. A decision, an opportunity, a step of faith that will soak everyone around you.

Is God calling you to that right now? If so, don't shrink back. That is not your overflow to manage. That is his.

~45 seconds

Where Is Your Splash Zone?

A spill of red wine on a white top.

Or perhaps it is more personal than that. Not a flood — but a mark. Something that has been noticed. A conversation that went further than you expected. A relationship where hope is quietly doing something neither of you fully planned.

You may feel a little exposed by it — like, did I just do that? Did that really come from me? Yes. It did. Because you were being filled. And the person on the other end of it will not forget it.

~1 minute

Where Is Your Splash Zone?

A splash of tea. A drop of water.

Or — and for many of us, most of the time, this is the truest answer — it is barely visible at all. A cup of tea carried down the hall, a little sloshing over the rim. Easily wiped. Nothing lingers.

But someone else did. A moment of patience you didn't plan. A word, quietly spoken, that landed somewhere you'll never know about.

And sometimes the person it lands on is someone you find difficult. The person who drains you. Who you'd cross the room to avoid. The God of hope doesn't ask for compatible people. He just asks for proximity. And then he spills — whether we planned it or not.

The cup was full. It spilled. That is the Spirit.

~1 minute

Most of us will leave today carrying a cup, not a log flume — and that is enough.

Because when a cup is full, it doesn’t stay contained.

The spill — the consequence — is also the point.

That spill might be this week — a small moment you almost missed.  It might be something God is quietly doing across the whole year, so gradual you only recognise it looking back.

The God of hope is still filling — right now, in this room. And the world is still waiting for hope to spill into it, from ordinary cups, carried by ordinary people.

This week, notice where the cup spills. That is where the Spirit is already at work.

Reflective Sending
~1 minute

So may the God of hope fill you, as you trust in him, with all joy and peace.

And may you overflow with hope, not by your effort, but by the power of the Holy Spirit.

And may we notice, in the days ahead, where the Spirit causes hope to spill even in the smallest of ways — especially toward the people we least expected.

Filled with joy.

Steeped in peace.

Overflowing with hope.

And the extraordinary thing is this — God has chosen to let his hope enter the world that way.

Amen.
